Summary of Request Evolution

  • Original Request (Oct 6): 20 in-vitro accessions (10 white/yellow, 5 orange, 5 purple) + botanical seed in all three colors.
  • Change #1 (Oct 19): No white botanical seed exists → dropped from seed request.
  • Change #2 (Oct 19): No purple botanical seed until 2022 → dropped from seed request. Botanical seed narrowed to orange only.
  • Change #3 (Oct 23): In-vitro total increased from 20 to 30 accessions (adding purple in-vitro from curator). Confirmed max allowed is 35.
  • Change #4 (Nov 16): I. setosa and I. nil (wild species for breeding) were in the spreadsheet but dropped — no breeding planned for now.
  • Change #5 (Apr 2): Only 11 of 15 botanical seed families available (4 missing) — final reduction at shipment time.
  • Recurring issues: Requester didn’t know how to use the ordering platform, asked about IDs. Handoff to a new person (Requester B) required re-explanation. Multiple clarification rounds about what material types were available vs. desired. Invoice/payment process confusion.

Why This Matters for AI-Assisted Germplasm Acquisition

  • 7 months from first inquiry to materials being ready — much of that time spent on back-and-forth clarification.
  • The requester’s original specifications (color, Brix levels, material type) did not match what was actually available, requiring multiple rounds of adjustment.
  • An AI system could have immediately cross-referenced the request against available inventory and presented realistic options upfront.
  • The curator, breeder, genebank manager, and coordinator all had to be manually looped in at different stages. An AI system could orchestrate these workflows automatically.
  • Platform confusion and procedural questions consumed significant email volume. An AI-guided interface could eliminate this entirely.
  • Even at the final stage, 4 of 15 seed families were unavailable — something that could have been flagged earlier with real-time inventory checking.
